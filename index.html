Jennifer's speech

Pam was my only sibling and we were close although like most sibling relationships there was complexity.

 I was with her a year and a half ago, when the oncologist told her what her choices were for treatment after being diagnosed. The options were pretty dreadful and the prognosis was grim. She choose no treatment and I agreed that for her, it was the right decision. She never waivered from that decision and she lived longer and more fully than anyone predicted. She swam, walked, played some golf and tennis and worked at her beloved bookstore through the Christmas season.  Her last months were tough for sure, and yet…

I have a distinct memory of standing with Pam, 20 years ago, the day after our mother died. We were behind our house in Florida. There was an overhang off the back of the house, with 20 orchids in hanging pots. I recall that as we looked back at the house, we both noticed  a stunning, beautiful little grass snake, emerald green with a white belly, was gracefully slithering from orchid pot to orchid pot. When the little snake came to last pot, he rested  his head on the petals of an orchid and basked in the sun. At that moment in my memory, Pam and I turned to each other and simultaneously said “MOM?”

This memory of a moment in time has always been, for me an important moment….of a connection with my sister.

Early in her journey  with her illness, I shared this memory of the snake and both of us saying to each other, “mom”. Pam looked at me incredulously and said…”but you weren’t there, I was alone!”

Pam lived her life feeling alone. Relationships were not easy for her and she could be prickly to protect herself.  During her illness, she did let some people in, and I feel fortunate to be one of those people. And for a person like Pam who had difficulty trusting,  she trusted me.  Over the course of her illness, with all the driving back and forth, working to find ways to make her life easier… many people said to me, oh you are such a good sister, she is so lucky to have you.  I can honestly say, although it was hard and at times, I worried I could not go the distance, the time was precious and filled with many gifts.

  • Pam and I became closer and despite the context, her illness, we had fun together: dinners, walks and talks. She was not alone, we were together. 

  • I witnessed how incredibly brave and what a strong spirit she had.

  • I was supported by  and became connected to many people here in Norfolk, connections I will treasure for the rest of my life. The list of people to thank is long and I will leave that to  one on one conversations for fear I might leave someone out.

Here are some things I would like you to know about Pam:

  • You may not know, that up until recently, Pam held the official record for catching the largest small mouth bass in North America-in a lake in Northern Michigan.

  • She once bought a cow, a little bull, who she named Hugo. She did not want him to be slaughtered for veal and she supported him for the rest of his life. He was a very charismatic cow and you will learn more about him from someone else, later today.

  • She loved dogs, nature, and water in all its forms-we shared this having grown up in Florida on a lake. Fresh lakes, ocean, snow, ice and yes even rain-she would have loved this summer!  She was determined to swim this summer, a wish that was not fulfilled.

  • She loved books and most of you know this, the play Hamilton, I don’t think there was a more fervent fan.

Pam’s birthday was June 5th.  She had surgery two weeks before, a colostomy, one of the many indignities that she courageously adapted to.  The week before her birthday, Madeline Falk, who took exceptional care of Pam her last few months called me.   “Jen, I think Pam should have a birthday party.”  I was skeptical .  

“Ok, let me ask her if she wants one since Pam is not a fan of surprises”.  

I called her that night and asked her…  

“Some folks want to throw you a birthday party.”    “ That would be nice is all she said.” 

And so once again Christopher and Betsy volunteered there home and hosted a small party.  Madeline, took her shopping and she bought a white skirt and a new shirt-not many people with a colostomy dress in white.  I was not able to come although I did get details from those who attended and from her the next day.   And there are pictures of her enjoying herself and putting her feet in the water,  a poor substitute for a swim, although better than nothing.  When I talked to her the next day she reported-

“That was the best day of my life!”

And although she did not say it specifically, I think I know, she felt loved, cared about and not alone!  

The party was on a Saturday. On Sunday she savored the memory of her party and by Monday she began a precipitous decline. On Wednesday Christopher and Madeline called me.

“Its time to come we think.”  

When I arrived, it was clear they were right.

She died peacefully at sunrise, exactly a week after her 70th birthday party.

She was home, she was not in pain, and she was not alone! 

I am a psychotherapist. I love deep conversations about difficult topics.  Pam and I never talked like that. Through her entire journey, I kept waiting for that moment when she would talk about what it was really like for her, I only got snippets, it was not her way.  One day last winter, when I was visiting, she announced from the next room…” Jen, I have something to tell you!”  I took a deep breath and braced myself, I thought this might be the moment she actually goes deeper.”  She marched into the kitchen and said…

”now when I am dead, and you are here cooking a bake potato, you need to remember…. my oven is faulty, and you have to turn the temperature up to 450 degrees.”  

I looked at her and just said 

“ok”  

And I don’t even like bake potatoes.

Luke's Speech

Hi everyone, thank you all for coming. 

I have to admit, this is the first memorial speech I’ve ever had to privilege of writing, and Pam (as some of you are aware) wasn’t the easiest person to write to/about even when she was alive, so I have been doubly challenged in drafting this. 

Some of my best stories and memories of Pam aren’t memorial material (or so my mother and googling “what not to say during a memorial tribute” tell me). That’s not to say these memories are lacking in love, kindness and joy but rather all are bursting at the seams with a shade of humor probably better left unsaid in tributes for the departed. 

In light of that, I won’t  be reminiscing about all the times Pam’s off color comments to my wife of asian descent francesca, or how she used to graze all the toppings off the dinner salad while instructing will and I about the the rules and etiquette of arranging silverware, or her whimsically offensive commentary during a Queer Eye for the Straight Guy episode. 

Pam’s complexity is evidenced in the fact that all those things are true, and we all still loved her deeply and (in 2-5 day increments) cherished her presence in our lives. She was the aunt who every Christmas for the past 30 years took pride and delight in arranging carefully selected gifts at the dinner table for every member of my family (usually magic trick trinkets or dollar snatchers). And when will and I brought home a 40ft Christmas tree it was Pam who applauded our efforts, seeing the christmas magic in us needing to run a complex cable system to ensure we all survived the holidays unsquished.

They say, or someone has said somewhere I’m sure, that our views of loved ones only gets richer and more complex after they cross the rainbow bridge. Like a book that keeps adding chapters after the prologue. For me, whats striking in retrospect and completely escaped me while she was alive was how incredibly punk rock Pam was throughout her life. Prickly, malcontent, r

esistant to letting people in and uninterested in changing her ways till the day she died. 

So here’s to the pam the enigma. To Pam who showed up to everyone of my terrible highschool plays (“well it couldn’t get any worse than opening night right?”, I can only imagine her craftily whispering to a proud parent sitting next to her). To Pam who relentlessly dotted on, supported, and bragged about will and I, through our occasional successes and many stumbles. 

Here’s to Pam, the prickly punk rock aunt i will miss dearly for all her quirks.

Will's Speech

Hi, my name is Will, and I’m Pam’s nephew and I’d like to share a few stories that I believe shine a true light on Pam.

When I was in elementary school Pam went through a rather nasty divorce. After some time had past, I felt that it was time she anti up again and find herself a new husband. Being 9 at the time I felt that I was very qualified to identify a suitor. So everytime I spotted some random guy on the street who looked the part, or someone who performed at our school I’d let Pam know. “Hey Pam, found the perfect guy”. However everytime I brought her Mr.Right she’d politely say, “Will,I don’t think he’s the one”. This went on for a while, and finally I asked her, “Pam what am I missing, can you give me an example of who you would consider?”, and without missing a beat she said, “Steve Martin would be good”. 

That was our Pam, celebrity status from the day she came into this world. And her runin with celebrities was not all that uncommon. On our weekly calls she would occasionally tell me about the time Robert Redford gave her the look when she was running through central park. Or how her bookstore patrons included the likes of Daniel Day-Lewis or Dustin Hoffman. Pam would be giddy as a school girl recounting the time Dustin left his grocery list at the cashier desk, and Pam swore she never even considered looking at it. He was very appreciative when she returned it to him. 

Pam loved books. She also loved recommending books that she never read. She was a voracious reader...when it came to reviews. Which made her an excellent gift giver. Every Christmas or birthday I knew I could count on Pam to deliver something extraordinary. Despite being an excellent gift giver, she was dreadful to buy presents for. Despite having a normal sized body, to this day I don’t think there is a single piece of clothing that our family gave her that she said fit. “My head’s too pointy for that hat”, was one of her favorite go to lines. This last Christmas though Luke had an idea. 

Pam was an avid NYT crossword puzzle enthusiast. One of Luke’s childhood friends, James, just so happens to write for the NYT crossword puzzles. So last Christmas he got James to craft a crossword puzzle, specifically curated for Pam. As a bonus we all created one for Pam as well. About a week after Christmas I gave her a call and she was ecstatic, she couldn’t believe it, and spent the next 20 minutes going over all the prompts with me. I asked her about our contributions as well, and with a wry smile behind her voice she just said, “Wilfered, you guys aren’t crossword people”. Vintage Pam. 

After I graduated college I made it a point to talk to Pam about once a week. I knew she was alone in a big house and appreciated a quick call. These calls were always relatively short, 15-20 minutes, and consisted mainly of me providing some updates on my life, and asking “What’s going on in Norfolk”, to which Pam would respond, “Nothing, absolutely nothing”. Which is odd because when I dug in a little deeper and asked “What are your weekend plans?”, she would say “Nothing Will, just nothing....well there is a dinner party at Christopher and Betsy’s, Co is coming over for a drink later, John Garrel wants to go for a walk, a tennis clinic Saturday morning, etc...you get the point. Despite having a loving community of people around her Pam loved to complain. But that’s not all she loved. 

  • She loved to swim in Toby pond and Doolitte lake, both Gilly and Dinah rose were excellent syncronized diving partners 

  • She loved to play sports, she was a beautiful athlete even in her later years

  • She loved cracking a dry jab and knew how to hit you right where it counts

  • Like the time I showed up with long hair and she gave me a hug and said, “it looks better shorter” 

  • She loved to tell me about how she was on a first name basis with Daneil Day Lewis 

  • She loved telling stories

  • Such as the time she was kid in florida and she still hasn’t forgiven my mom for roping her into an adventure into an alligators nest to snatch up a baby alligator 

  • She loved Dinah Rose and Gilly, and had a habit of treating her dogs like royalty, giving them ice cream and fromage as a pallet cleanser after dinner 

  • She loved walking on the paths in her backyard and finding the little whimsies that she and neighbor hid for each other

  • She loved my mother, despite never really saying it, other than “I’m glad you’ll be around after I’m dead because this house is gonna need some looking after”


Pam was an incredible lady. A celebrity in our eyes, and someone I’m going to dearly miss. She was dealt a hard hand at the end of life, but she handled it with the grace and class that is definitely worth a date with Steve Martin.
